For my senior engineering thesis project, I built a custom-designed, research-grade air sampling instrument for research in the Amazon rainforest. The instrument was attached to a Matrice 600 drone from DJI, and I had the opportunity to travel to Manaus, Brazil to carry out multiple test flights from atop a research observation tower in middle of the rainforest. I was also awarded the Dean's Award for Outstanding Engineering Projects.




Project Abstract

Produced mainly through natural emission from vegetation (~10^15 grams a year), volatile organic compounds (VOCs) influence climate through their involvement in photochemistry, such as the production of ozone in the presence of NOx and light. Decades of stationary tower-based studies (10s of meters) and aircraft flights (10s of kilometers) have revealed extreme heterogeneity in time and space. However, the actual variation in forest cover occurs at 1 km or less, a scale that represents a missing link in advancing our present-day understanding of VOCs in atmospheric chemistry. This project involves the engineering design, production, and validation of a drone-deployed VOC sampling instrument. The instrument design includes a custom pump and selector valve system for sequentially collecting samples through sorbent-packed thermal desorption tubes at pre-defined GPS waypoints. The instrument features a lightweight, robust enclosure that can be integrated directly to the chassis of existing drone platforms and is also enabled to collected flow data during sampling routines. Once deployed as part of a multi-drone fleet from a mobile boat platform, this sampling instrument will help elucidate uncertainties surrounding the magnitudes, variations, and processes controlling VOC emission and uptake at previously unexplored spatial and temporal scales.

Remote-Controlled ATV


Full Design/Specifications Documentation

Description

Working on a team of four mechanical engineers, I designed and constructed an ATV using two AC motors from an electric screwdriver and other custom fabricated parts. Our design is based on a two-wheel drive system positioned in the front and back of the ATV, allowing for a zero degree turn radius. The drive wheels were connected to two larger wheels by treads to traverse obstacles that required climbing. Both drive wheels were powered by a gear train involving 24, 44, and 96-teeth gears, designed in a lateral assembly with a 1:128 gear ratio. A high gear ratio was selected in order to maximize the torque of the system, which would later prove to be beneficial for scaling the ramp and other obstacles on the competition course. The original design for the block and unhitching mechanism involved a “push-cart” design, although this method was eliminated in later design iterations due to logistical problems with climbing the ramp. The final design called for placing the block on top of the ATV, which stabilized the vehicle’s center of mass, and allowed for a successful performance at the competition.

Vibration Test Mechanical Fixture


Description

While working as a Build Reliability Intern at SpaceX, I helped the avionics department design a mechanical fixture to perform vibration tests on pressure transducers to be installed on the upcoming Dragon V2 Crew Spacecraft. All pressure transducers must undergo vibration tests that simulate the physical forces of spacecraft launch and flight. The vibration test machine is monitored by closed-loop accelerometers. My fixture accomodates multiple configurations of test transducers and accelerometers. The design required understanding the mechanics of installation (cantilever, clamped, etc.), how closed-loop feedback systems work, and physical design constraints of the testing machinery.

  • Date: August 2015
